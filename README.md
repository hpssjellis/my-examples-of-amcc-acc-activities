The Anterior Mid-Cingulate Cortex (aMCC) is a critical brain region with diverse functions essential for survival, decision-making, and social behavior.

Core Functions
Pain and Negative Emotions: The aMCC is a central hub for processing and experiencing pain (both physical and social/emotional), and strongly registers negative emotions such as fear, disgust, anger, and sadness. It contributes significantly to the affective (emotional) component of pain.

Motivation and Reward (Value-Based Decisions): It is fundamentally involved in reward-based decision-making and goal-directed behavior. The aMCC tracks the magnitude and probability of potential rewards and costs, helping to calculate the effort required for a goal versus the potential benefit (effort-based decision-making).

Cognitive Control and Effort: This region is a key player in cognitive control, particularly when a task requires significant concentration, mental effort, and error detection. It is highly engaged during difficult tasks, when monitoring conflicts (e.g., in a Stroop task), and when there is a need to change strategies or adjust behavior (behavioral adaptation).

Stress and Autonomic Regulation: As a core component of the central autonomic network, the aMCC plays a crucial role in calibrating the body's physiological responses to stress, helping to integrate emotional, cognitive, and physical responses to threat or demanding situations.

Motor Control: It contributes to the initiation of voluntary movements and the selection of appropriate motor actions in service of a goal.

Social Processing: The aMCC is active during complex social interactions, such as those involved in empathy, social evaluation, and fairness assessment (e.g., its robust activity during the Ultimatum Game reflects the processing of unfairness).

Relationship between aMCC and ACC
The Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC) is the broader, larger brain region situated on the inner wall of the frontal lobes. It is generally recognized for its role in emotion, pain processing, and cognitive control.

The aMCC is a specific subregion of the ACC, located in its middle (mid-cingulate) part.

In some neuroanatomical models and research, the aMCC is also commonly referred to as the dorsal ACC or caudal ACC due to its location (closer to the top/back of the ACC) and its functional overlap, particularly with respect to cognitive and motor control functions.
